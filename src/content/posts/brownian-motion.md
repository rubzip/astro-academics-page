# Brownian Motion: The Visibility of the Invisible

The random, zigzagging motion of microscopic particles suspended in a fluid—first observed by botanist Robert Brown in 1827—remained a mystery for decades. In this work, I propose that this "Brownian motion" is not a property of the particles themselves, but rather the result of constant collisions with the fast-moving atoms or molecules that make up the liquid.

## The Molecular-Kinetic Theory

By applying the principles of statistical mechanics, we can derive the mathematical laws governing these fluctuations. If we assume the liquid is composed of discrete molecules in constant thermal motion, then any suspended particle will experience a series of random impulses.

While each individual collision is negligible, the statistical imbalance of these collisions over small time intervals leads to a perceptible displacement.

## The Diffusion Coefficient

The most significant result of this analysis is the relationship between the mean squared displacement $\langle x^2 \rangle$ of a particle and the time $t$:

$$\langle x^2 \rangle = \frac{RT}{N} \frac{1}{3\pi\eta a} t$$

Where:
- $R$ is the gas constant.
- $T$ is the absolute temperature.
- $N$ is Avogadro's constant.
- $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity of the liquid.
- $a$ is the radius of the particle.

## Definitive Evidence for Atoms

This formula provided a revolutionary way to calculate Avogadro’s number and, for the first time, gave the scientific community a method to "count" atoms by observing the macroscopic behavior of visible particles. It effectively transitioned the atomic hypothesis from a useful mathematical shorthand to a proven physical reality.
