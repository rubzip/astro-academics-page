# A Technical Expert Third Class

I have finally secured a position at the Swiss Patent Office in Bern. For many, this might seem like a mundane bureaucratic detour, but for me, it has become a "secular cloister"—a place of solitude and intellectual freedom.

## The Art of Precision

Evaluating patent applications requires a unique kind of discipline. One must strip away the legal jargon and the inventor's enthusiasm to find the core "technical thought." I spend my days analyzing complex mechanical devices, ensuring their claims are rooted in physical reality. This rigorous practice of critical thinking is, in many ways, identical to the way I approach a theoretical problem in physics.

> "When you have to think and explain exactly what an inventor is trying to achieve, you learn to see the essence of a physical system."

## Thinking in the Gaps

The office provides me with a stable income and, more importantly, a schedule that leaves my mind undisturbed during the long, quiet intervals between applications. While the world sees a clerk reviewing blueprints for synchronizing clocks or improving elevator brakes, my mind is often drifting toward the nature of light and the paradoxes within Maxwell's equations.

The clock towers of Bern, which I see every day on my way to work, have become silent witnesses to my inquiries into the relativity of time. Perhaps it is fitting that a place dedicated to the practical synchronization of time should be the birthplace of a theory that proves time itself is not absolute.
